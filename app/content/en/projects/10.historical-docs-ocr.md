## About the Project

The History Department at MIPT is conducting a large-scale effort to digitize archival collections from pre-revolutionary Russia — governor reports and statutory charters from the 18th-19th centuries. The documents are stored as handwritten and printed scans, often with damage, faded ink, and non-standard typefaces. Manual transcription of a single document takes an archivist anywhere from several hours to an entire workday.

Our task was to build an ML pipeline that would automate text recognition on these scans and dramatically accelerate the archivists' work. The main challenge: standard OCR solutions (Tesseract, EasyOCR) are virtually useless with pre-revolutionary orthography, handwritten script, and the atypical layout of historical documents.

## Architecture

The pipeline is built as a sequence of three stages, each solving its own task:

**1. Text block detection.** The input is a document scan, often of uneven quality — with stains, creases, and ink smudges. The YOLOv8 model locates text regions on the image and separates them from decorative elements, seals, signatures, and background artifacts. To train the model, over 2,000 images were annotated using Roboflow, marking text blocks of different types: headings, body text, and marginalia.

**2. Segmentation and preprocessing.** Detected text regions are cropped from the scan and undergo normalization: skew correction, binarization, and noise removal. This is critically important for historical documents where contrast between text and background is often minimal. OpenCV is used for adaptive binarization and morphological operations that restore readability even on heavily damaged fragments.

**3. Text recognition.** Preprocessed fragments are fed into a seq2seq model based on Hugging Face Transformers, fine-tuned on a corpus of pre-revolutionary texts. The model accounts for the peculiarities of old orthography (yat, fita, yer) and handles both printed and handwritten text. The result is transcribed text mapped to coordinates on the original scan.

The entire pipeline runs through JupyterLab, allowing archivists to visually inspect each processing stage and make corrections when necessary.

## MLOps

Working with historical data requires strict experiment tracking — each region has its own handwriting characteristics and document conventions. ClearML was used for tracking all training runs: comparing metrics across iterations, model versioning, and logging hyperparameters. Roboflow provided a unified environment for annotation and dataset version management — when adding new documents from another region, we could fine-tune the model without losing previous results.

## Tech Stack

**ML:** PyTorch 2.2 (CUDA 11.8), Ultralytics (YOLOv8), Hugging Face Transformers 4.37, Datasets, scikit-learn

**CV:** OpenCV, Roboflow, Supervision

**MLOps:** ClearML, JupyterLab 4.1

## Key Achievements

- **mAP50 93%** on text block detection — the model confidently finds text even on damaged scans
- **2,000+ scans** annotated and processed from archives of 29+ regions spanning 1804-1882
- **~10x speedup** — automated document transcription instead of manual archivist work
- **Working with a unique domain** — pre-revolutionary orthography and handwritten text, inaccessible to standard OCR solutions
